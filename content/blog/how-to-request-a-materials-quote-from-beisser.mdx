A materials quote from a lumberyard is not the same thing as a hardware-store price check. If you are planning a new home, remodel, addition, deck, siding replacement, or window package, a good quote usually involves more than pulling a shelf price on a single item. It often means reviewing plans, identifying product categories, checking branch routing, and making sure the scope actually matches what the job needs.

That is why the best Beisser quote requests include context. The clearer the project information is at the start, the easier it is for the team to route your request to the right branch and respond with something useful instead of a rough guess.

## Why a Beisser quote is different

A lumberyard quote often includes takeoff work, package coordination, and product guidance. Instead of quoting one board or one window size in isolation, the team may be helping with a framing package, a full window schedule, a deck material list, or a siding assembly tied to trim and accessory details.

That difference matters. A simple unit price may not answer the real question if the job also needs lead-time guidance, delivery planning, substitutions, or branch-specific follow-up. For contractors, that package approach can save time during bidding. For homeowners, it helps turn a general idea into a more realistic project conversation.

## What to bring or send

The best quote requests usually include at least some of the following:

- A plan set, sketch, or marked-up drawing
- Square footage or dimensions
- The project type: new construction, remodel, addition, replacement, or repair
- Product categories involved, such as lumber, windows, doors, decking, siding, millwork, or engineered wood
- Your preferred branch, if you already have one
- Any brand preferences you already know

If you do not have full plans, that is okay. A simple sketch with dimensions and a short project description is still better than a one-line message that just says "Need a price."

## What the quote process typically looks like

Most quote requests start with basic routing. Beisser needs to know which branch should handle the request and what type of project it is. From there, the team can determine whether the scope is a straightforward material request or something that needs estimating, design help, or a specialist in a category like engineered wood, windows, or decking.

A typical quote may include:

- A materials list or package direction
- Estimated delivery or pickup considerations
- Notes on special-order items or custom configurations
- Lead-time guidance for products that are not standard stock
- Follow-up questions if more detail is needed before pricing is finalized

Turnaround can vary based on scope. A simple material request may be faster than a plan-driven package with windows, doors, or engineered wood components. The fastest way to help the team turn it around is to send enough information up front.

## Four good ways to request a quote

### 1. Use the online quote form

The online form is the cleanest option when you want your request routed to the right branch without guessing who to contact. It is especially useful if you already know your category, branch preference, or project type.

### 2. Call your branch

If the scope is time-sensitive or you want to talk through the request first, calling the branch is a good move.

- **Fort Dodge:** (515) 573-4166
- **Birchwood / Johnston (Showroom):** (515) 986-4422

A branch call can help determine whether your request belongs with inside sales, estimating, showroom support, or a service workflow.

### 3. Walk in

Some projects are easier to explain in person, especially when samples, drawings, or finish decisions are involved. The Birchwood / Johnston showroom can be especially helpful for windows, doors, and finish-related categories, while branch counters are often the fastest way to start a more straightforward material conversation.

### 4. Upload plans with your request

If the project needs a takeoff or a more complete package, send the plans or note that they are attached. A framing package, deck package, window schedule, or siding quote is much easier to scope when the team can review the drawings instead of working from partial measurements.

## A simple way to speed things up

If you want the quote process to move faster, include three details in your first message: what you are building, where the project is located, and what documents you can share. That gives the team enough to route the request well and ask better follow-up questions if needed.

The goal is not just to get a number. It is to get a useful quote that reflects the actual project.
